Chess is a game which appears to be increasingly complex the more you get into learning it. From my perspective, just the thought of playing higher rated players who knew exponentially more about openings and strategy than I did made it appear that catching up or even beating them once was futile. I went into exploring this dataset to dispel this myth and to hopefully reassure other novice chess players that there was less to learn and memorize (at least with regards to openings) than they mght think. My hypothesis is that most of the popular openings contain a relatively small number of moves and that lower rated players defeating higher rated players happens often regardless of the differential.

The first topic we are looking at is openings. Chess.com highlights how important openings are for beginners to learn and that chess isn't just a memorization contest. The graphs below display the data from a Lichess dataset found on Kaggle. The first relationship we will be exploring is between the number of opening moves and the type of opening.

Now, the next relationship we will be exploring is between game outcomes and the difference in ratings between two opponents.
